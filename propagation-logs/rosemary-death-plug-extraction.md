### 🪓 Autopsy of a Nursery Plug

Because nurseries frequently oversow seeds or strike multiple cuttings into a single cell to create a "fuller" retail pot, your green stems and the dead woody structure are entirely separate individual clones. 

Since the primary woody clone was strangled by its original non-biodegradable mesh wrap or plastic "death plug," and its entire upper canopy is completely desiccated, **its lifecycle is 100% spent**. It will not produce new growth from the root zone. In rosemary, once the vascular cambium at the root crown fails completely, there is no mechanism for subterranean adventitious suckering.

```
   [Substrate Surface]
      │           │
      ▼           ▼
  (Living)     (Dead)
  Green Stems   Woody Stem
      │           │
      │           └───> [Flush Cut at Substrate] ──> Crown Dies Back Completely
      │
      └───> [Healthy Root Expansion Zone]
```

#### Action Plan:
*   **The Cut:** Do not try to yank the dead plant out by the roots, as its root system is likely intertwined with the living stems inside the substrate matrix. Pulling it risks tearing the fragile root hairs of the surviving green rosemary. Instead, take your snips and execute a clean, flush cut exactly at the substrate level. Over time, the subterranean dead wood will naturally break down without harming the neighbors.
*   **Top Dressing Strategy:** Applying a 100% inorganic grit top-dress (such as coarse quartz sand, chicken grit, or fine pumice) is highly recommended. It will **not** prevent the living stems from expanding or pushing out new low-level lateral growth. Mechanically, it will keep the upper crown completely dry, insulate the remaining root zone from surface-dwelling fungus gnat larvae, and prevent the substrate from splashing onto the low-hanging green needles during watering.

### 🥬 Arugula: The Case for Keeping the Air Gap

Your current mechanical choice to leave the reservoir base off and keep the arugula container elevated on blocks is the superior setup for this phase of growth. 

```
[Arugula Planter Bottom] ──> [Perforations Open to Air] ──> [Air Velocity Prunes Roots]
▲
└─── [Elevated on Blocks] ──> (No Stagnant Humidity Zone)
```

By leaving the bottom open to the room's ambient air currents, you are maintaining a clean **air pruning zone**. When downward-seeking taproots hit those bottom perforations, the low humidity causes the root tips to naturally dehydrate and die back. This mechanical stop forces the plant to aggressively send out lateral, horizontal secondary roots throughout the core substrate, building a hyper-dense, highly efficient root matrix. 

If you reattach the reservoir base now—even without water in it—you close that gap, trap evaporation from your pump-sprayer passes, and create a stagnant, high-humidity boundary layer. The roots will grow straight through the holes and coil along the plastic floor, becoming lazy and susceptible to rot. 

**The Verdict:** Keep it elevated on the blocks. Since you are targeted-misting with a pump sprayer and haven't hit structural runoff yet, there is zero benefit to changing the configuration. If you eventually do get minor runoff as the canopy demands heavier watering, simply slide a completely separate, shallow cookie sheet or utility tray *underneath* the blocks, leaving the air gap intact.

### 🌿 Basil: The Passive Sub-Irrigation (SIP) Conversion

Unlike arugula's shallow, fast-cycling canopy, basil develops an aggressive, deep, high-transpiration root system that is perfectly suited for a true Sub-Irrigated Planter (SIP) configuration. 

Since the original manufacturer provisions for water transit are gone, you can easily retro-engineer this into a passive capillary wicking system using your **nylon rope**:

```
[Basil Substrate Matrix]
  │   ▲              ▲
  │   │ (Capillary)  │ (Capillary)
  ▼   │  Up-Draw     │  Up-Draw
[Screen Liner]───────[Screen Liner]
      ║              ║
      ║ (Nylon Rope) ║ (Nylon Rope)
      ▼              ▼
[=========== Reservoir Base ===========]
[~~~~~~~~~~~~~ Water Level ~~~~~~~~~~~~]
```

#### Mechanical Implementation:
1.  **The Wick:** Cut two or three lengths of your nylon rope. Nylon is a synthetic polymer that exhibits excellent capillary draw via surface tension while remaining completely impervious to fungal rot or structural degradation under anaerobic conditions (unlike cotton).
2.  **Threading:** Thread the ropes up through the bottom perforations of the main container so that at least 3 to 4 inches of rope sit vertically inside the lower root zone substrate. 
3.  **The Drop:** Let the remaining lengths of rope dangle straight down into the rectangular end sections of the reservoir base. 
4.  **The Action:** When you fill the base through the central spout, the nylon will passively draw moisture upward into the substrate via capillary action based entirely on the plant's transpiration rate. This keeps the root zone consistently damp but perfectly aerated, avoiding the need to mess around with active aquarium power heads.
