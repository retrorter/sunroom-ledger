### 🌶️ Jalapeño Basal Trim Evaluation

Looking closely at `jalapeno-basal-trim.jpg` and `jalapeno-basal-trim-0.jpg`, those four remaining leaves are clean and healthy, but they are sitting directly inside the primary splash zone. 

If your core objective for this basal trim is to maximize bottom airflow and eliminate the vector for soil-borne pathogens splashing up onto the foliage, you can actually be significantly more aggressive:

* **Clear the Splash Zone:** Once your upper canopy has a few solid tiers of mature leaves, those lowest four leaves can be entirely removed. Ideally, you want the bottom 4 to 6 inches of the main trunk completely bare.
* **Axillary Node Suppression:** In `jalapeno-basal-trim.jpg`, you can see tiny new vegetative axillary shoots (suckers) already waking up and pushing out right at the joints where the old leaves meet the stem. If you want a clean, single-stem trunk at the base, pinch those tiny green nodes out early with your fingernails before they woody up into secondary branches.

### 🧸 Kalanchoe Fuzzy Propagation Setup

The execution in `kalanchoe-super-fuzzy-chunky-mix.jpg` is textbook. Settling those highly pubescent succulent leaves (*Kalanchoe tomentosa*) into a bone-dry, ultra-coarse matrix of chunky mix and perlite is the safest way to trigger root development.

* **Ballast Mechanics:** Using small pea gravel as top-layer ballast is an excellent engineering choice here. It provides the necessary mechanical resistance to anchor the light, top-heavy leaves vertically without compressing the highly aerated root-zone footprint below.
* **Hydration Halt:** Staying completely disciplined on the "no water" protocol is 100% correct. Until these leaves differentiate cells to push adventitious roots, they have zero vascular capacity to drink soil water. Any ambient moisture around the base right now would simply invite opportunistic fungal rot. They will thrive entirely on their internal parenchymal moisture reserves for the next few weeks.

### 🌱 Arugula Micro-Canopy & Thinning Timeline

The visual data in `arugula-day-6.jpg` and `arugula-day-6--stem-density.jpg` shows an incredibly uniform, high-density germination carpet. The cotyledons are fully deployed, and while the stems are expectedly elongated due to the heavy seed distribution pattern, they are holding strong structural turgor pressure.

* **The Shear Delay:** Waiting 24 to 48 hours for your Chikamasa scissors to arrive is completely fine. At Day 6, the seedlings are just beginning to compete for lateral root space and light, but a two-day delay will not cause irreversible lodging (falling over) or damping off. 
* **Tool Choice:** While your unused ceramic electronics snips have the necessary precision tips to work the grid, holding out for the specialized horticultural blades of the Chikamasas is the preferred move. High-carbon steel or specialized ag-blades slice through plant tissue cleanly with a single shear motion, whereas general-purpose or bypass tools can pinch and bruise the delicate stems of neighboring seedlings you intend to keep. 

Once those shears arrive, you can systematically drop into that dense matrix and clear out a checkerboard pattern to let the remaining crowns expand.
