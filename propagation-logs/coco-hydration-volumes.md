### 📊 Volumetric Reality Check

Opting for the lower end of the range (**3 gallons of water per bucket**) is the exact right tactical adjustment. Processing 1 kg (two of your 1.1 lb blocks) per 5-gallon bucket will work perfectly, but you will be operating at near-maximum volumetric capacity. 

Here is how the physical space breaks down inside the bucket during execution:

*   **Dry Displacement:** Two compressed blocks take up roughly 0.5 gallons of physical space. 
*   **Initial Fluid Height:** Adding 3 gallons of water brings your initial starting volume to roughly **3.5 gallons**, leaving you about 1.5 gallons of headroom at the rim.
*   **Fully Expanded Volume:** A standard 1 kg block of compressed coco chips typically expands to roughly 14–15 liters of loose media, which translates to just under **4 gallons of total volume**. 

Because the coco chips completely absorb the water as they swell, your final result will be a bucket filled roughly 80% of the way to the top with damp, hydrated chips. 

---

### 🛠️ The Only Two Cavents to Watch For

1.  **Tight Stirring Clearances:** Because the expanded chips will sit just a few inches below the bucket rim, manual agitation will be a bottleneck. When you go to stir and break up the core of the blocks after the initial 20-minute soak, do it slowly so you don't pitch chunks over the edge.
2.  **The Hydration Gradient:** With less excess water pooling at the top, the top block might draw up moisture faster than the bottom block can register it. To counter this, flip the blocks halfway through the soak if you notice the core of one is still holding its compressed shape.

Going with 3 gallons prevents the mixture from turning into a floating, unmanageable soup. If the chips still feel a bit rigid or dry after they absorb the initial pour, you can easily top them off with an extra quart from your Eley sprayer right before you dump them onto the black plastic envelope for the solar dry phase.
