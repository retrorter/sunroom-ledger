### Axil / Axillary :: Anatomy
The precise upper angle or junction where a leaf petiole, lateral shoot, or pedicel meets the main supporting stem. Anatomically analogous to a plant's "armpit," it contains the axillary meristem tissue responsible for generating lateral branches, secondary foliage, and nodal flower buds.

Regarding your pepper observations, here is the botanical ground truth for *Capsicum* development:

#### The Shared Architecture
Biologically, your **Jalapeño**, **Armageddon**, and **Sweet Banana** peppers all share the exact same underlying reproductive design pattern: **sympodial development** and **dichotomous branching**. 

1. The plant grows a single main stem up to a certain point.
2. The shoot apical meristem (the absolute growing tip) transitions and terminates by forming a flower bud. 
3. Further vegetative development then continues from the uppermost **axillary meristems** right below that terminal flower, which grow outward to form the classic "Y" fork.

Because of this repeating sequence, **every single pepper bloom and subsequent fruit develops at an axillary junction or fork**, regardless of the variety. They are structurally solitary and axillary, arising directly at those nodal points.

#### Why the Sweet Banana Pepper Looks Different
Your visual tracking is highlighting a macroscopic difference in how the mature fruit presents, rather than a fundamentally different nodal architecture:

*   **Jalapeños & Armageddons:** These varieties often produce tighter internodes at the upper crown, making the blossoms look deeply nestled or clustered right inside the tight axillary leaf junctions.
*   **Sweet Banana Peppers:** These plants tend to have slightly more elongated internodes and highly flexible, elongated pedicels (the individual fruit stems). As the heavy, elongated banana pepper fruit develops, its sheer physical mass forces the pedicel to hang completely pendant, swinging downward away from the central node. 

When you see a large banana pepper hanging low, it can easily look like it emerged from an independent lateral fruiting branch. However, if you trace that thick green pedicel back to its mechanical anchor point, you will find it terminates right at a classic axillary fork.
